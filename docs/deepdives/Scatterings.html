Physics behind the Sunyaev-Zel'dovich effect

Single electron-photon scattering

In this section, we inspect the electron-photon scattering process. At the smallest scale, the SZ effect comes from single electron-photon scatterings and as such, it is good to investigate this part a little bit.

In[1]:
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as pt

import MockSZ.Models as MModels

%matplotlib inline

num_arr = 3000  

s = np.linspace(-1, 1.5, num=num_arr)                                                                                                                                     
beta = np.array([0.01, 0.02, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5])                                                                                                                                                            

simObj = MModels.ScatteringKernels()

fig, ax = pt.subplots(1,1, figsize=(10,5))

for i in beta:
    amplitudes = simObj.getSingleScattering(s, i)  
    ax.plot(s, amplitudes, label=r"$\beta$ = {}".format(i))
ax.set_ylim(0, 12)
ax.set_xlim(-1, 1.5)
    
ax.set_ylabel(r"$P(s;\beta)$")
ax.set_xlabel(r"$s$")
ax.legend(frameon=False, prop={'size': 13},handlelength=1)

pt.show()

The curves represent the scattering probability $P(s;\beta)$: the probability that an electron with velocity $\beta = v / c$ undergoes a scattering interaction with a photon at frequency $\nu_0$, such that the photon has a new frequency $\nu$, averaged over all photon incidence angles $\mu$ with respect to the electron's velocity vector. This frequency shift is expressed by the logarithmic frequency shift $s=\ln(\frac{\nu}{\nu_0})$. It is important to note here that the integral over interaction angles assumes an isotropic illumination of the electron. In reality, the CMB illuminating the electron is anisotropic and any bulk motion of the gas containing the electron will introduce a Doppler shift of the CMB in the electron's rest frame. This last effect is taken into account in MockSZ by introducing analytical cross-terms, in addition to the scattering terms treated in this notebook.

Note how the scattering probability grows wider and more asymmetric for increasing $\beta$, showing a higher probability for upscattering. This is due to the higher electron velocity, which increases he probability of upscattering a photon, and relativistic beaming, which increases the spectral range of photons as seen in the electrons rest frame.

The electron distributions

Now, having discussed the single electron-photon scattering case, we can discuss electron distributions. Since a radiation field may interact with electrons at different velocities, the case of scattering of a photon off an ensemble of electrons needs to be implemented. The first distribution we encounter is a Maxwell-Juttner distribution, also known as a relativistic thermal distribution.

In addition to a thermalised distribution, MockSZ can also simulate a non-thermal distribution, the powerlaw distribution.

Photon scattering from an electron population

Now that we have discussed the single electron-photon scattering scenario, it is time to discuss the case of photon scattering from an electron population. The difference is that instead of a single $\beta$, an electron population contains a continuous range of $\beta$'s. This is reflected by including the electron distribution in the total probability. It should be noted that each scattering probability shown here is for a single scattering per photon only. This amounts to us setting $\tau_\mathrm{e}=1$.

We start by showing scattering probabilities for a relativistic Maxwellian distributions of electrons.

In[19]:
Te = np.array([5.1, 15.3, 45.9])
s_arr = np.linspace(-1.6, 2.5, num=1000)  

fig, ax = pt.subplots(1,1, figsize=(10,5))

for i in Te:
    amplitudes = simObj.getMultiScatteringMJ(s_arr, i)
    ax.plot(s_arr, amplitudes, label=r"$T_e$ = {} KeV".format(i))
ax.set_ylim(0, 5)
ax.set_xlim(-1.6, 2.5)
    
ax.set_ylabel(r"$P_1$")
ax.set_xlabel(r"$s$")
ax.legend(frameon=False, prop={'size': 13},handlelength=1)

pt.show()

It can be seen that the higher the electron temperature $T_\mathrm{e}$, the broader the distribution becomes. This is expected from the Maxwellian distribution, which gets broader with increasing $T_\mathrm{e}$, allowing for larger values of $\beta$, which in turn broadesn the single electron-photon scattering kernel.

It is immediately visible that the upscattering tail ($s>0$) is a lot longer than the thermal scattering kernel. This causes the ntSZ signal (see the tutorial on single-pointing signals) to also extend to higher frequencies compared to the tSZ signal.
